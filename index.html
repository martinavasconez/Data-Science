Project Goals

Have you ever wondered if expensive cosmetics are actually safer for your health? In this project, we aim to answer this question using a data-driven approach.

We will explore the chemicals and substances commonly found in beauty and cosmetic products. Our objective is to analyze how these chemicals are priced and their potential effects on human health. This will help us determine if higher-priced products are genuinely better for consumers' healthor if the perception of safety is just an illusion.

Why does this matter?

  • For consumers: Price often plays a big role in purchasing decisions. People tend to associate higher prices with better quality, especially in the cosmetics industry.
  • For health: Chemicals in cosmetics can cause bodily irritations and other health problems. Understanding the relationship between price and chemical composition can help consumers make safer choices.

Our Hypothesis:

If certain beauty products are priced higher, then the chemicals in them should cause fewer bodily irritations and health problems. Expensive products give consumers the impression that they are better for human bodiesbut is this really the case?

How we'll explore this:

To support our analysis, we will gather data from relevant datasets that illustrate the relationships between product pricing, chemical composition, and associated health impacts.

By analyzing this data, we hope to uncover meaningful insights into the safety of cosmetics and the factors that influence consumer perception.

Key Datasets for Investigation

Our analysis utilizes three primary datasets that address key aspects of our exploration into cosmetic chemicals and their implications for human health:

Cosmetics Dataset: This dataset provides detailed information about various cosmetic companies, their products (categorized by type), ingredients, and prices. It serves as the foundation for analyzing trends in pricing, ingredient composition, and product categories.

What we analyze from this dataset:

  • Which products contain specific chemicals?
  • How are prices distributed across brands and product types?
  • Are higher-priced products associated with specific ingredients or categories?

ECHA Europa C & L Inventory: This database is sourced from ECHA Europa and focuses on the hazard classifications of individual chemicals. Each chemical is linked to a unique CAS Number and is categorized based on hazard notifications from companies and manufacturers.

What we analyze from this dataset:

  • The frequency and severity of hazard notifications.
  • Patterns between chemical hazards and product pricing.
  • Specific hazard codes related to skin, eye, respiratory irritation, and environmental toxicity.

Environmental Working Group (EWG) Skin Deep Cosmetics Database: This database provides comprehensive ratings for common cosmetic chemicals based on factors like carcinogenic potential, immunotoxicity, and reproductive toxicity. This dataset is particularly valuable for building our model, as it provides health ratings that are relevant to the recreational use of cosmetics.

We will use this database after determining the average prices for chemicals to extract their comprehensive health rating and build our model. The health hazards found here are more relevant to recreational use, whereas the ECHA Europa C&L Inventory provides a more general overview of hazard data for all types of use and is better suited for preliminary analysis. This database is also an excellent resource for consumers looking to research products before buying them.

# Function to fetch and clean data from a given URL
def fetch_and_clean_chemical_data(url):
    try:
        response = requests.get(url, timeout=10)
        response.raise_for_status()

        # Parse the HTML content
        soup = BeautifulSoup(response.content, 'html.parser')
        html_tables = str(soup.find_all("table"))
        df = pd.read_html(StringIO(html_tables))[1]  # Select the relevant table

        # Clean the dataset
        df.columns = df.columns.droplevel(0)
        columns_to_drop = [
            "Supplementary Hazard Statement Code(s)",
            "Specific Concentration limits, M-Factors",
            "Notes",
            "Classification affected by Impurities / Additives",
            "Joint Entries",
            "Unnamed: 11_level_1"
        ]
        df = df.drop(columns=columns_to_drop, errors='ignore')  # Ignore errors if columns are missing
        df.columns = [
            "Hazard Class and Category Code(s)",
            "Hazard Statement Code(s)",
            "Hazard Statement Code(s), Extended",
            "Pictograms, Signal Word Code(s)",
            "Additional Notified Information",
            "Number of Notifiers"
        ]
        return df
    except:
        return None

Comparing Hazard Distributions Across Price Categories

In this section, we examine the aggregated hazard notifications across the four price categories: Lowest Price, 25th Percentile, Median Price, and Highest Price. By summarizing the total number of hazard notifications and visualizing the distribution of hazard codes (e.g., H302, H412) for each category, we aim to uncover trends and differences in chemical risks associated with varying price ranges.

However, since each price category contains a different number of ingredients, comparing the total number of hazard notifications directly could lead to biased conclusions. To address this, we normalized the hazard data by dividing the total number of hazard notifications for each hazard code by the number of unique ingredients in that price category. This normalization ensures a fair comparison by representing the average number of notifications per ingredient, rather than the raw totals.

This analysis allows us to explore whether higher-priced ingredients are associated with fewer or less severe health hazards, or if risks persist across all price tiers. Understanding these patterns provides valuable insights into the relationship between product cost and safety, helping to answer critical questions, such as whether higher prices translate to safer products or if lower price ranges consistently feature riskier chemicals. Ultimately, this comparison contributes to a deeper understanding of the potential trade-offs between ingredient cost and consumer health, informing both consumer decisions and industry practices.

Building the K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) Model to Predict EWG Rating

Lastly, we developed a K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) model to predict the EWG Rating of an ingredient based on its price. This approach helps us explore whether ingredient prices can serve as reliable predictors of safety ratings. To build this model, we followed these key steps:

  1. Trained the model using the price feature to predict the EWG Rating.
  2. Created a new series to specify the price input for predictions
  3. Standardized the data to ensure consistent scaling
  4. Calculated the Euclidean distance between data points using 30 neighbors, a balance chosen to avoid underfitting (too few neighbors) or overfitting (too many neighbors)
  5. Extracted the EWG Rating corresponding to the chosen price based on the nearest neighbors

By using this model, we aim to uncover whether a simple feature like price can effectively predict hazard ratings, providing valuable insights into the relationship between cost and safety in cosmetics.
