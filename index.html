Project Goals¶

For our final project, we, Disha Amin and Martina Vasconez, will collaborate to explore the chemicals commonly found in the beauty and cosmetic industry. We aim to analyze how these chemicals are priced and their potential effects on human health.

Our hypothesis is that higher-priced beauty products contain chemicals that are less likely to cause bodily irritations and other health issues. This is based on the premise that consumers often perceive more expensive products as having safer and healthier ingredients.

To support our analysis, we will gather data from relevant datasets that illustrate the relationships between product pricing, chemical composition, and associated health impacts.

Preliminary Datasets¶

Our three preliminary datasets reflect each component of our exploration topic concerning the chemicals in cosmetics and their implications for human health.

  • Cosmetics Database: We discovered a dataset on Kaggle that contains information about various cosmetic companies, their products (along with respective categories), ingredients, and prices. This dataset serves as a crucial starting point for our analysis of chemicals in cosmetics. We aim to answer key questions, such as which products contain specific chemicals and which chemicals are associated with higher prices. Additionally, while it may not directly relate to our hypothesis, observing price trends among different brands will provide valuable context. Overall, this dataset will offer insights into brand identities, the chemical compositions of their products, and their pricing strategies.

  • EPA Chemical Database: The second dataset is sourced from the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA). This database features a search function that allows users to explore datasets related to specific chemicals and their associated hazards. Each chemical is identified by a unique chemical ID. For our project, we will focus on datasets related to various chemicals' hazards, genotoxicity, and potential for skin/eye irritation. For instance, we identified Titanium Dioxide from the first dataset and retrieved relevant information about its skin and eye irritations. This includes details on various irritation tests, the body part tested (skin or eye), the animal subjects used, and the final irritant classification. This dataset will help us explore how common chemicals in cosmetics and beauty products affect human health.

  • Product Usage and Demographics Dataset: The final dataset examines the relationships between cosmetic products, their usage frequency, and their target demographics (men or women). By integrating this dataset with the first two, we can investigate which gender is more willing to spend on cosmetics and self-care items and whether these items have specific health impacts related to gender. While this dataset may not be as critical to our primary analysis, it provides supplementary data that can lead to more in-depth observations about trends in the beauty industry.

Data Exploration and ETL¶

We will begin our analysis with the cosmetics dataset to gain insights into the beauty industry. This dataset consists of 11 columns, including Label, Brand, Price, and Name. Our initial focus will be on exploring this dataset in depth to provide context and understand its structure. Through this analysis, we aim to derive insights into various aspects of cosmetics, such as price distribution, product rankings, and other relevant trends.

In the future, we plan to integrate this dataset with the EPA chemical dataset to enhance our analysis of chemicals present in skincare products. This combined approach will allow us to investigate the relationships between product characteristics and their chemical compositions, thereby offering a comprehensive understanding of how these factors influence consumer choices and health outcomes.

This scatter plot illustrates the relationship between two key variables in our dataset: price and rank. Interestingly, the graphic suggests that, counterintuitively, higher prices are associated with lower product ranks.

The correlation coefficient calculated between these two variables is -0.01858, indicating a very weak negative relationship. This means that there is almost no linear correlation between price and rank, suggesting that variations in price do not significantly influence product rankings.

To deepen our understanding of this relationship, it would be beneficial to have documentation outlining the factors that contribute to a product's rank. This information could clarify whether certain price ranges are systematically associated with specific qualities or attributes that influence consumer ratings. For instance, the observation that products tend to be ranked higher when they are cheaper raises questions about consumer perception and behaviorspecifically, whether lower-priced items are perceived as providing better value or quality in the cosmetics market.

Overall, while our initial hypothesis suggested a potential positive correlation between higher prices and better rankings, the data indicates that this may not hold true in this context, highlighting the complexity of consumer preferences in the beauty and cosmetic industry.

Analysis by Ingredient¶

Since our observation question revolves around product composition, after conducting preliminary analysis on the given data, we modified the DataFrame to see the relationship between ingredients and price. The original DataFrame grouped all ingredients for a given product into 1 value, so we need to tidy the data.

First, we'll drop Rank and Skin Type, which are irrelevant columns for this analysis. Then, we'll modify the DataFrame and set the index as individual ingredients.
